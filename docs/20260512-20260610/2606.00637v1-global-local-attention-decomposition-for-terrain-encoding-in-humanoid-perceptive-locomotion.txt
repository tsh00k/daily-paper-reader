Abstract —Although reinforcement learning has significantly advanced humanoid locomotion, perceptive policies still struggle on sparse-foothold terrain and constrained environments. Success in these scenarios requires both broad terrain awareness and precise foothold selection, two perceptual roles that conventional encoders often entangle. To address this challenge, we propose Global-Local Attention Decomposition (GLAD) for terrain en-coding in humanoid locomotion. Realized by a coarse-to-fine encoder over a robot-centric elevation map, GLAD explicitly separates these objectives: a global attention branch utilizes attention pooling to summarize the surrounding terrain context, while a state-conditioned local attention branch sparsifies and encodes precise foothold-relevant geometry. This explicit atten-tion decomposition prevents the dilution of fine-grained spatial cues while reducing training overhead. Experiments demonstrate that GLAD enables reliable locomotion over challenging gaps, stepping stones, and stairs. Furthermore, the learned policy exhibits emergent terrain-responsive behaviors, autonomously following narrow paths and avoiding obstacles under simple velocity commands without explicit navigation planners. In real-world deployment on a Unitree G1 humanoid robot using onboard LiDAR, the proposed method achieves robust zero-shot sim-to-real transfer across diverse sparse-foothold and obstacle-rich domains. 

I. INTRODUCTION Humanoid robots, owing to their anthropomorphic body plan and legged mobility, are a promising platform for op-erating in environments designed for humans and adapting to diverse terrain conditions [1], [2]. Reinforcement learning (RL) has recently driven substantial progress in humanoid locomotion, and even “blind” policies can now achieve ro-bust walking over a wide range of ground conditions [3], [4]. Nevertheless, reliable locomotion on complex terrain remains challenging because the robot must simultaneously perceive surrounding geometry, make terrain-aware decisions, and maintain robust control under limited stability margins [5], [6]. This challenge becomes especially pronounced on sparse-foothold terrain, where success depends on identifying feasible support regions and placing the feet precisely [7]. To handle such terrains, perceptive locomotion policies must incorporate exteroceptive observations such as elevation maps or depth images. While essential, these raw observations are high-dimensional and noisy, which can reduce robustness, increase computational cost, and complicate policy training [8]. Attention mechanisms offer a principled way to fuse proprioception with exterocep-tion: guided by the current motion state, they can empha-size locomotion-relevant terrain regions and thereby support general, interpretable terrain encoding [13]. Building on this idea, AME [9] introduces an attention-based map encoder that uses multi-head attention (MHA) to aggregate local terrain features conditioned on proprioception, enabling implicit and precise foothold selection; AME-2 [14] further augments this design with additional global context. To address this issue, we propose Global-Local Attention Decomposition (GLAD) for terrain encoding in humanoid locomotion. Realized by a coarse-to-fine encoder, GLAD explicitly separates the extraction of broad context from fine-grained foothold selection. A convolutional neural network (CNN) first extracts spatially aligned local features from a robot-centric elevation map. Subsequently, a global attention branch utilizes attention pooling to summarize the surround-ing terrain context, while a local attention branch performs state-conditioned sparsification and MHA to encode precise, foothold-relevant local regions. Without explicit attention su-pervision, these two branches learn complementary behaviors: the global attention focuses on key terrain regions ahead of the robot, whereas the local attention highlights foothold-relevant regions. This decomposition enables GLAD to combine broad terrain-context understanding with precise foothold-oriented focus, while avoiding the dilution of fine-grained local ge-ometry and reducing the cost of applying dense attention to the entire local feature set. Beyond sparse-foothold traversal, GLAD significantly en-hances terrain-responsive locomotion in highly constrained environments. Guided solely by simple velocity commands, the learned policy interprets terrain geometry to autonomously follow narrow paths and avoid nearby obstacles, exhibiting emergent local-navigation behaviors without requiring hierar-chical planners. We extensively validate the proposed method in both simulation and real-world experiments on a Unitree G1 humanoid robot. The policy demonstrates robust zero-shot sim-to-real transfer across diverse discrete terrains and obstacle-rich domains, relying entirely on onboard LiDAR-based elevation mapping, as shown in Fig. 1. II. RELATED WORK 

A. Learning-based Humanoid Locomotion 

Recent advances in learning-based humanoid locomotion have been enabled in part by scalable training pipelines inherited from quadruped locomotion, which support massive simulation parallelization and reliable policy transfer to real robots [15]. With only proprioceptive sensing, “blind” policies can already achieve robust walking over uneven ground, sim-ple obstacles, and external disturbances [3], [4]. For more chal-lenging terrain, however, exteroceptive perception becomes in-creasingly important because the robot must anticipate hazards before contact and adapt its motion to surrounding geometry [16], [17], [18]. Existing perceptive locomotion methods mainly rely on depth images or elevation maps [19]. Depth images are ap-pealing for deployment, but in learning-based pipelines they often require costly rendering or additional temporal modeling to compensate for occlusion and limited field of view [6], [11], [12]. Elevation maps provide a more structured robot-centric description of local terrain geometry and can be generated efficiently in simulation or constructed on hardware from depth cameras or LiDAR [5], [8], [10], [19], [20], [21]. For sparse-foothold locomotion tasks, elevation maps are still often needed as privileged information during training or as an intermediate representation in the depth images pipeline, because they provide precise terrain geometry that is critical for accurate foothold reasoning [6], [12], [22]. 

B. Locomotion on Sparse Footholds 

Discrete terrains with sparse footholds highlight the mobil-ity advantage of legged robots, but they are also among the most challenging settings for perceptive locomotion, especially for humanoids. Model-based methods explicitly plan feasible foothold sequences within hierarchical perception–planning– control pipelines, achieving precise motion on sparse supports but remaining limited by model accuracy and robustness to perception and control errors [23], [24]. End-to-end RL has become a dominant direction due to its robustness and strong real-world performance, yet accurate foothold placement on complex terrain remains difficult to guarantee. A key bot-tleneck is terrain encoding: encoders based on multilayer perceptrons (MLPs), CNNs, latent-variable models directly compress elevation maps or depth images, often overlooking the terrain details and motion-state relevance required for foothold decisions. Therefore, prior learning-based methods for sparse footholds often rely on terrain-specific curricula, expert policies, or carefully designed foothold rewards [7], [22], [10], [11], [12], which are difficult to engineer, inject strong human priors, and can bias policies toward conservative or terrain-specific foothold choices. State-conditioned attention offers a promising alternative for implicit foothold planning. By fusing proprioception with exteroceptive terrain features, attention can focus on regions most relevant to the current locomotion decision and provide a more precise, general terrain representation [13]. D. Global-Local Terrain Encoding 

As illustrated in Fig. 2, the elevation-map input is first processed by the proposed GLAD encoder to form a compact terrain representation. This representation is then concatenated with proprioception and fed into the policy network. 1) Spatially Aligned Local Feature Extraction: Given the input elevation map mt ∈ RL×W ×3, we first employ a two-layer CNN to extract spatially aligned local features. The 3D coordinates at each map location serve as the three input channels. Unlike AME [9], which convolves only the height values and then concatenates the original 3D coordinates with the CNN output to inject positional information, we directly convolve the 3D coordinates so that positional cues are embedded in the learned features from the beginning. Both convolutional layers use zero padding to preserve boundary information and maintain spatial alignment with the underlying terrain map. This spatial correspondence is important because the subsequent global and local attention modules should remain grounded to physical map locations. Without zero padding, the link between learned features and terrain locations becomes weaker, which negatively affects the final policy performance [9]. The first convolutional layer performs strided downsampling with a stride of s. Unlike AME [9], which preserves a one-to-one correspondence between CNN features and the original scan points, our encoder decouples terrain sensing resolution from encoded feature resolution. The resulting features remain aligned with the original elevation map, but on a coarser grid of size (L/s ) × (W/s ). This design shortens the local feature sequence, reduces the computational cost of the subsequent attention modules, and allows higher-resolution elevation maps to be used as input while maintaining a similar encoded feature resolution for downstream processing. The second convolutional layer projects the downsampled features into a unified embedding dimension D, which is shared by the proprioceptive embedding, the surrounding terrain context vector, and the MHA components. The CNN thus outputs a feature grid Kgrid ∈ R(L/s )×(W/s )×D . These local features encode both terrain geometry and positional information around each retained map location. This is impor-tant for foothold selection, since a feasible foothold depends not only on local terrain geometry but also on whether the corresponding location is reachable by the robot. 

2) Global-Local Attention Decomposition: After local fea-ture extraction, we construct the GLAD terrain encoding in three steps. First, a global attention branch utilizes attention pooling to aggregate all local features into a compact surround-ing terrain context vector. Second, a local attention branch fuses this context vector with a proprioceptive embedding to generate a state-conditioned query vector, which is used to score and sparsify the local features by retaining only the top-

K candidates. Third, the local attention branch applies MHA over these retained sparse features to produce a fine-grained, foothold-relevant terrain encoding. IV. EXPERIMENTS 

A. Training and Experimental Setup 1) Network Hyperparameters: We train the policy using proximal policy optimization (PPO) [25]. The actor and critic share the same terrain encoding module and use separate MLPs with hidden dimensions [512 , 256 , 128] . The elevation map covers a 1.6 m × 1.0 m robot-centric area at 0.05 mresolution, yielding an input grid of L = 33 and W = 21 .The proposed GLAD architecture uses a two-layer CNN with zero padding throughout; the first layer performs strided downsampling with kernel size 5, stride s = 2 , and 16 output channels, yielding a (L/s ) × (W/s ) = 17 × 11 downsampled feature grid with an effective resolution of approximately 

0.1 m. The second layer uses kernel size 3 and outputs to a unified embedding dimension D = 64 . This dimension 

D is consistently used for the proprioceptive embedding, the surrounding terrain context vector c, the query vector q, and the MHA feature spaces. The MHA module is configured with

2) Two-Stage Training: Following prior AME-based train-ing practice, we adopt a two-stage training procedure to gradually improve both locomotion performance and terrain representation quality [9]. In the first stage, the policy is trained on basic terrains with perfect terrain perception, which initializes the terrain representation module and allows the controller to acquire fundamental locomotion skills from accu-rate terrain observations. The terrain set in this stage includes conventional perceptive-locomotion terrains such as stairs, random grid terrain, random rough terrain, and slopes, together with stepping-stone-everywhere and gaps. In the second stage, we retain stairs and gaps, while replacing the easier stepping-stone-everywhere terrain with more challenging sparse-foothold courses, as illustrated in Fig. 3. These include parallel-row, single-row, and alternating stepping-stone layouts, together with rails and terrains with randomly distributed cylindrical obstacles. As a result, the policy must learn not only precise foothold placement but also terrain-responsive motion adjustment for crossing and avoiding obstacles. Within each stage, we further employ a progressive terrain curriculum with ten difficulty levels: robots that successfully traverse their assigned terrain are promoted to a higher level, whereas failed robots are moved to a lower level. 

3) Baselines and Ablations: We compare the proposed GLAD architecture with two baseline terrain encoders and two ablation variants. The first baseline is AME [9], which introduced attention-based map encoding for perceptive lo-comotion and serves as the primary reference baseline. The second baseline is AME with global context , which follows the terrain encoder design used in AME-2 [14]. In this variant, an additional MLP followed by max pooling is applied to the CNN-extracted local features to produce a global feature, which is concatenated to both the MHA query and the encoder output. The added MLP uses hidden dimensions of [256 , 128] .To isolate the contribution of each component in the pro-posed GLAD architecture, we further evaluate two ablation

4) Training Details: We use only standard locomotion rewards and avoid handcrafted foothold rewards or terrain-specific shaping. Observation noise and domain randomization are introduced mainly in the second stage, including per-step observation noise, random scan drift in the terrain map, artificial pushes, and randomization of torso mass and contact friction coefficients, without terrain-specific tuning. Together, these settings highlight that sufficiently informative terrain representations can support sparse-foothold and other complex terrain locomotion without specially tailored reward design, thereby helping isolate the contribution of terrain representa-tion quality to locomotion performance. All methods are trained in simulation in NVIDIA Isaac Sim and use the same robot model, observation setting, terrain cur-riculum, reward design, domain randomization, and optimiza-tion protocol. Unless otherwise noted, encoder components common to all methods, such as the CNN feature extractor, use identical hyperparameter settings, and each method is trained through massive parallelization with 4096 robots for two stages of 10 ,000 iterations each.

C. Simulation Experiments 1) Unseen Terrain Performance: Since the proposed method and the baselines solve the training terrains reli-ably, we evaluate them on four strictly unseen, 10 m long-horizon test terrains as shown in Fig. 5. Terrains (a) and (b) are composite and randomized single-row stepping-stone courses with a top surface area of 20 × 20 cm 2, tested at a commanded forward velocity of 1.0 m/s. Terrain (c) contains randomly distributed pentagonal pillars, imposing a heavier burden on identifying precise foothold candidates from cluttered geometry. Terrain (d) forms a narrow sinusoidal path requiring extended deviation from the nominal heading to remain traversable. In all terrains, the desired heading points along the positive x direction, and the yaw-rate command is generated by multiplying the heading error between the robot and the desired heading by 0.5. For Terrains (c) and (d), the commanded forward velocity is reduced to 0.8 m/s to allow more directional adjustment. Table I reports the overall success rates, averaged over 64 parallel terrain instances and 

512 robots for each terrain type. On stepping stones such as Terrains (a) and (b), all methods generally perform well. However, GLAD w/o global attention 

falls below 90% , confirming that relying solely on fine-grained foothold-relevant features is insufficient. Removing the surrounding terrain context degrades performance even on terrain layouts that are structurally similar to the training set. A bigger performance gap emerges on Terrains (c) and (d). In Terrain (c), where feasible support regions are dense but ambiguous, the full GLAD model seamlessly combines foothold-level precision with surrounding terrain context to maintain a success rate above 90% . By contrast, AME with global context performs noticeably worse because its single MHA module forces the entanglement of the surrounding terrain context and precise foothold-relevant geometry, which dilutes attention concentration. Similarly, GLAD w/o local attention suffers a severe performance drop, demonstrating that surrounding terrain context alone is insufficient to resolve the precise foothold geometry required in highly cluttered environments. Terrain (d) represents the most demanding setting, requiring anticipatory directional adjustments prior to reaching infeasi-ble regions. Under this scenario, GLAD substantially outper-forms all baselines. The ablation results show that removing the local attention branch fails to yield sharp foothold cues, while removing the global attention branch strips away the contextual forward traversability needed for sustained path following. These findings confirm that the surrounding terrain context feature c and the foothold-relevant feature f fulfill complementary roles. By explicitly decomposing them, GLAD avoids architectural entanglement and leads to more robust, terrain-responsive legged locomotion.

3) Interpretable Attention Decomposition: To better un-derstand the attention mechanism, we visualize the global and local attention weights across various terrain layouts in Fig. 6. The resulting distributions exhibit a distinct functional separation between the two branches. The global attention produces terrain-dependent, broadly receptive patterns. On flat ground, the weights lean toward the far end of the observation map. On stairs, gaps, and obstacle-rich environments, the attention maintains distant awareness while increasingly highlighting geometric edges with signifi-cant height variations. On more constrained terrains, such as stepping stones and narrow paths, it concentrates on nearby forward-traversable regions. This adaptability demonstrates that the surrounding terrain context feature c effectively cap-tures anticipatory cues regarding upcoming geometric changes and forward traversability. Conversely, the local attention yields strictly concentrated spatial patterns. Guided by state-conditioned sparsification, the retained local features concentrate primarily on feasible foothold regions relevant to the robot’s current motion state. The subsequent MHA weights typically peak at the robot’s current or next foothold locations. This highly localized be-havior confirms that the fine-grained, foothold-relevant feature 

f directly provides the spatial geometry required for precise foot placement. Collectively, the quantitative alignment analysis and visu-alizations validate the architectural motivation of GLAD and elucidate its performance gains. Compared to AME [9] and 

AME w/ global context , the explicit global-local decomposition yields more concentrated, terrain-adaptive, and interpretable attention profiles while preserving the surrounding terrain context needed for robust locomotion. 

D. Real-world Deployment 

We deploy the proposed method on a Unitree G1 humanoid robot. The robot weighs 35 kg, stands 1.32 m tall, and has 29 

actuated degrees of freedom. For exteroceptive perception, it is equipped with a Livox Mid-360 LiDAR that provides point clouds together with synchronized inertial measurement unit (IMU) measurements. For deployment, we use FAST-LIO [26] to fuse LiDAR and IMU measurements for state estimation, and construct a robot-centric gridded elevation map from the fused point cloud [27]. The system runs on a laptop equipped with an RTX 5090 Laptop GPU, where both the elevation-mapping module and the control policy operate at 50 Hz. We test the policy on four representative real-world terrains. The first is an alternating stepping-stone terrain, where each stone has a top surface of 20 ×25 cm and the spacing between two same-side stones is 70 cm. The second is a staircase terrain, where each step has a tread depth of 30 cm and a height of 12 cm; a 30 cm gap is inserted between the ascending and descending stair segments to form an unseen composite terrain. The third is a wide-gap terrain with a gap width of 

70 cm, which exceeds the maximum training gap of 50 cm and approaches the forward sensing limit of the elevation map. The fourth is an obstacle-rich terrain, where wooden blocks and suitcases are placed along the route, requiring the policy to adjust its motion online over a short horizon to avoid collisions while maintaining forward progress. As shown in Fig. 1, the proposed method achieves zero-shot sim-to-real transfer and generalizes successfully across all tested scenarios. V. CONCLUSIONS We proposed GLAD, a coarse-to-fine terrain encoder for humanoid perceptive locomotion. By separating global terrain-context aggregation from state-conditioned local foothold en-coding, GLAD reduces attention entanglement, lowers compu-tational cost, and yields interpretable attention patterns aligned with support regions. Simulation and real-world deployment on a Unitree G1 humanoid show that GLAD enables robust locomotion over sparse stepping stones, wide gaps, and obstacle-rich terrains, with zero-shot sim-to-real transfer using only onboard LiDAR-based elevation mapping. The learned policy also exhibits terrain-responsive behaviors such as narrow-path following and obstacle avoidance under simple velocity commands. Limitations remain in the local elevation-map sensing range, reconstruction noise, and the computational overhead of real-time elevation mapping.
