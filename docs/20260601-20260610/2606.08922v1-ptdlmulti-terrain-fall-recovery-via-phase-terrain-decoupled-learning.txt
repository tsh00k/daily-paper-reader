# PTDL: Multi-Terrain Fall Recovery via Phase–Terrain Decoupled Learning 

Xiaoyu Xu, Zhiming Chen, Yuenan Zhao, Ran Song and Wei Zhang 

Abstract — Humanoid robots can fall on slopes, gravel, and uneven ground in unstructured environments. We target inte-grated fall recovery and locomotion: rebuilding balance from a fallen state using proprioception alone and resuming velocity-commanded walking at the fall site. Prior methods often stop at quasi-static rise, neglect the post-fall ground-contact phase, or, when trained on mixed terrains without separating recovery and locomotion phases or per-surface constraints, collapse to a single compromise get-up across surfaces. We propose Phase– Terrain Decoupled Learning (PTDL), which decouples training supervision along phase and terrain axes while deploying one proprioceptive policy. On the phase axis, projected-gravity-gated dual motion-prior discriminators and a probe-to-walk transition link post-fall recovery to commanded walking. On the terrain axis, terrain-stratified recovery shaping assigns surface-specific training supervision on flat ground, gravel, and slopes; terrain labels are training-only and withheld from policy observations, enabling implicit post-fall strategy selection at deployment. We validate PTDL on a 29-DoF Unitree G1 across flat ground, gravel, and slopes up to 20 ◦ in simulation and on hardware, achieving stable cross-terrain recovery, smooth recovery-to-locomotion transitions, and differentiated post-fall rise behaviors under one deployed policy. 

I. INTRODUCTION Humanoid robots operating in unstructured environments can fall on slopes, gravel, or uneven ground beyond labora-tory flat floors [1], [2]. When recovery fails, manual interven-tion interrupts mission execution and may cause secondary damage. Sustained autonomous operation therefore depends not only on fall avoidance, but on reliable fall recovery and subsequent task resumption—rebuilding balance at the fall site using proprioception alone and resuming velocity-commanded walking [3], [4]. This work targets integrated multi-terrain fall recovery and locomotion: the robot must recover after falls on multiple surface types, not merely stand up on flat laboratory floors. Fall recovery is substantially harder than steady-state locomotion: after impact, the robot lies in a multi-contact configuration and must establish viable support, raise its center of mass, and restore balance [5], [6], [7]. Surface type adds a further layer of difficulty because post-fall contact and stability requirements vary across flat ground, gravel, and slopes. A behavior that rises reliably on flat terrain may lose footing on gravel or drift downhill on slopes before balance is restored, and a policy trained toward one terrain-agnostic get-up rarely remains both safe and

Beyond terrain variation, the overall pipeline spans two dynamical regimes with distinct supervision needs: post-fall ground-contact recovery is multi-contact and aperiodic, whereas velocity-commanded walking relies on periodic foot contacts and velocity regulation. The core challenge is therefore to decouple phase and terrain-family heterogeneity during training while deploying a sin-gle, terrain-label-free proprioceptive policy that implicitly selects surface-appropriate post-fall behavior from contact and inertial history. Prior work covers only part of this coupled problem. Model-based and scripted methods construct get-up motions via contact graphs or trajectory optimization [2], [5], [6], but offer limited online transition to commanded walking on unseen surfaces. Reinforcement-learning methods train feedback policies from randomized fallen initializations [2], [3], [7]; many stop at quasi-static rise without recovery-to-locomotion, and multi-terrain training via domain ran-domization often still yields one terrain-agnostic get-up rather than multiple post-fall recovery modalities within one policy. HumanUP [3] demonstrates multi-surface get-up on real hardware, yet does not target unified recovery-to-locomotion under velocity commands, nor the “terrain-differentiated training / blind implicit selection at deploy-ment” design. Recent work unifies walking, running, and fall recovery on flat ground through state-dependent adversarial motion priors [8], mainly addressing phase/mode conflict but not post-fall recovery strategies that change on gravel and slopes. Terrain-aware upright walking relies on elevation maps or privileged exteroception [4], [9], [10], and terrain-specialized locomotion routes observations to deployment-time experts [11]; neither provides blind, single-policy, multi-terrain post-fall recovery. To address these limitations, we propose Phase–Terrain Decoupled Learning (PTDL), a training framework for inte-grated multi-terrain fall recovery and locomotion that exports a single proprioceptive actor πθ at deployment. PTDL fol-lows one organizing principle: decouple supervision along phase and terrain during training and unify behavior in one blind policy at deployment, rather than collapsing both heterogeneities into a single undifferentiated objective. On the phase axis, we combine reinforcement learning with two semantically distinct human motion libraries— post-fall get-up clips and velocity-conditioned locomotion clips—and inject both into the same actor through adver-sarial style supervision. Episode resets synchronize the active reference stream with the inferred phase so that each rollout receives matched prior supervision at the appropriate stage. On the terrain axis, our multi-terrain contribution is terrain-stratified recovery shaping combined with privi-leged training supervision and blind deployment, rather than domain-randomized compromise motions, deployment-time policy switching, or exteroceptive terrain classification. Training proceeds on a mixed curriculum over flat ground, gravel, and slopes; each parallel environment receives a terrain-family label ξ at reset that enters only training-time reward shaping and event logic, not the actor observation. The same πθ thus internalizes multiple post-fall recovery skills: gravel environments inject multi-point bracing and anti-roll objectives plus slow dual-foot probing after rise; slope environments inject anti-slide and lateral-support ob-jectives; flat environments inject efficient rise-to-walking ob-jectives; all complemented by contact-induced stability terms inspired by support-polygon reasoning [1]. A brief probe-to-walk transition further bridges recovery and commanded walking. PTDL is trained through a multi-stage curriculum that progressively introduces velocity commands, morphology coordination, dual-discriminator bridging, and mixed-terrain finetuning; only the final actor is deployed. The main contributions are: 1) We formalize PTDL, treating motion phase and terrain family as two orthogonal axes unified under single proprioceptive-policy deployment. 2) We propose terrain-stratified recovery shaping for multi-terrain fall recovery: training-time, ξ -conditioned post-fall supervision on different surfaces with observation exclusion at deployment, enabling one policy to learn and implicitly select multiple post-fall recovery modalities—distinct from domain-randomized compromise motions and deployment-time expert routing. 3) We propose a projected-gravity-gated dual-discriminator mechanism (Φfall and velocity-conditioned Φwalk ) that decouples recovery and locomotion style supervision within one network, linking post-fall recovery to commanded walking without separate deploy-time controllers. 4) We validate PTDL on a Unitree G1 humanoid (29 DoF) across flat ground, gravel, and slopes (5 ◦–20 ◦) in simulation and on hardware, with ablations evaluating the contribution of dual-discriminator routing, terrain-stratified shaping, and recovery–locomotion phase de-coupling. II. RELATED WORK 

A. Humanoid Locomotion Learning 

Reinforcement learning with domain randomization has become a dominant paradigm for legged and humanoid locomotion. Methods pioneered on quadrupeds [9], [11], [10] and extended to humanoids [12], [13] achieve robust velocity tracking on flat ground, slopes, and stairs through large-scale parallel simulation. Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP) [14] replace hand-crafted kinematic rewards with data-driven style regularization: a discriminator distinguishes reference motion transitions from policy rollouts, pulling behavior toward natural reference statistics. Recent unified controllers extend AMP with phase-dependent routing. Lu et al. [8] use projected-gravity gating to send fallen-state transitions to a recovery discriminator and upright transitions to a velocity-conditioned locomotion discriminator, enabling walking, running, and flat-ground fall recovery within one policy without runtime mode switching. That design pri-marily resolves style-gradient conflict between get-up and locomotion on a single surface; it does not treat terrain family as an orthogonal training axis nor provide terrain-differentiated post-fall recovery on gravel or slopes. 

B. Terrain Adaptation 

Blind policies trained with proprioception alone rely on domain randomization and curriculum learning to traverse heterogeneous terrain without elevation maps or depth sens-ing [13], [11]. Perceptive pipelines incorporate depth images, height fields, or privileged terrain information to improve foothold selection and slope traversal [15], [16], [9], [4]. Within AMP-based locomotion, MoRE [17] activates gait-specific discriminators under explicit gait commands and employs a mixture-of-experts actor, but depends on depth observations and targets periodic gaits rather than post-fall ground-contact recovery. More broadly, terrain specialization at deployment—via expert routing or terrain classification— adds switching complexity and does not by itself yield single-policy fall recovery whose rise behavior adapts to gravel, flat ground, and slopes. To our knowledge, no prior work combines terrain-stratified recovery shaping with phase-decoupled AMP discriminators to learn multiple post-fall recovery modalities under one proprioceptive policy.

A. Experimental Settings a) Implementation: Optimization is carried out via PPO [19] across 4,096 parallel environments at 50 Hz, with a five-frame observation stack (learning rate 3 × 10 −4,clip ratio 0 .2, GAE λ =0.95, discount γ=0.99, minibatch size 4 ,096, 5 optimization epochs per rollout). Simulation gravel is modeled as a flat surface with contact friction sampled from U (0.3, 0.6) and random surface-height noise (±0.02 m); slopes are planar inclines at fixed angles. Domain randomization perturbs base mass ( ±15%), joint damping, and contact friction. 

b) Metrics.: We evaluate three complementary dimen-sions of performance: 1) Success rate (SR, %): fraction of episodes in which base height exceeds the walk gate z⋆=0.72 m (Eq. (4)) for at least 1 s and the robot subsequently tracks velocity commands for at least 5 s without a secondary fall or out-of-bounds termination. 2) Time-to-stand (TTS, s): elapsed time from episode start to the first timestep with base height above z⋆;reported for successful episodes only. 3) Time-to-fall (TTF, s): interval from the first timestep above z⋆ to the next fall event; episodes that reach the timeout limit (240 steps = 4.8 s) without a subsequent fall are right-censored at 4 .8 s. Unless specified, simulated trials cover flat ground, gravel, and slopes at 5 ◦, 10 ◦, 15 ◦, and 20 ◦. Each configuration is trained with five random seeds; results are reported as mean ± std across seeds, with 200 evaluation episodes per seed per terrain.

VI. CONCLUSIONS We present the PTDL, a framework that unifies multi-terrain fall recovery and velocity-commanded locomotion in one humanoid policy by decoupling training supervision along motion phase and terrain family. We highlight two findings. 1) Phase heterogeneity is mitigated by projected-gravity-gated dual motion-prior discriminators that separate recovery and locomotion priors within one actor. 2) Ter-rain heterogeneity is mitigated by terrain-stratified recovery shaping with training-only labels withheld from policy obser-vations, enabling implicit, surface-adaptive post-fall behav-ior without domain-randomized compromise motions. Two limitations remain. 1) Get-up priors are mainly flat-ground LAFAN1 references; highly deformable, wet, or very steep surfaces are not yet covered, and hardware tests are limited to moderate slopes. 2) Deployment relies on proprioception alone and does not use vision or tactile sensing.
